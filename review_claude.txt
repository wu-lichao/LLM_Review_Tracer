Review of "Steering MoE LLMs via Expert (De)Activation"
(1) Summary
This paper introduces SteerMoE, a framework for controlling the behavior of Mixture-of-Experts LLMs at inference time by identifying and selectively activating or suppressing behavior-linked experts. The core idea is to compute a "Risk Difference" score for each expert by comparing activation rates across paired inputs exhibiting contrasting behaviors (e.g., safe vs. unsafe responses). At inference time, router logits are adjusted to promote or suppress targeted experts without any weight modification. The authors evaluate on two behavioral dimensions — RAG faithfulness and safety — across 6 MoE models and 11 benchmarks.
(2) Strengths
The approach is elegantly simple and requires no fine-tuning or architectural changes, making it genuinely lightweight. The dual framing — both improving safety and demonstrating that safety can be catastrophically degraded — is an important contribution to alignment research. The finding that combining SteerMoE with existing jailbreak methods like AIM can reduce GPT-OSS-120B's safety from 100% to 0% is striking and significant. The use of control benchmarks (MCTest, Fluency, Harmless) to verify that steering doesn't degrade general capabilities is methodologically sound. Cross-lingual transfer results (Table A.3) strengthen the generalizability claims. The interpretability findings — that behavior-relevant experts cluster in middle layers and align with token-level semantics — are a valuable secondary contribution.
(3) Limitations
The method requires white-box access to router logits, which limits applicability in API-only deployment scenarios. The hyperparameter selection (how many experts to activate/deactivate) relies on grid search and varies substantially across models, raising questions about reliability in new settings. The safety detection phase uses teacher-forced completions rather than free generation, which the authors acknowledge but could be a concern for how well detected experts generalize. The paper focuses on two behavioral dimensions; it is unclear how well the approach scales to more subtle or emergent behaviors where contrastive paired data is harder to construct. Finally, the "deactivation is preferable" finding, while practically useful, suggests the activation intervention is fragile, which somewhat limits the positive steering use case.
(4) Questions for the Authors
Could you elaborate on the computational overhead of the grid search for expert count selection? Is there a principled way to determine this without benchmark evaluation? For the cross-lingual transfer results, were the detected experts truly language-agnostic, or did their effectiveness degrade monotonically with linguistic distance from English? Given that unsafe routing paths survive post-training alignment, do you have evidence about when during training these paths form — are they a pretraining artifact or do they emerge from RLHF? Have you explored whether SteerMoE's identified experts overlap with those found by other interpretability methods (e.g., probing classifiers)?
(5) Score: 7/10
A solid paper with a clean idea, strong empirical results, and important safety implications. The main limitations are the white-box access requirement and the reliance on hyperparameter search, but neither undermines the core contribution.
